Soju

Soju, a clear distilled spirit, has a rich history deeply rooted in Korean culture. Its origins date back to the 13th century during the Mongol invasions, when the technique of distillation was introduced to the Korean Peninsula. Initially known as "arak" or "arakju," it evolved into the modern soju we recognize today. Traditional soju was made from grains like rice, barley, and millet, reflecting the agricultural abundance of Korea. During the Joseon Dynasty (1392-1897), soju gained popularity as a drink of the elite, used in royal ceremonies and rituals. However, it became more widely accessible in the 20th century when a rice shortage led to the government promoting alternative ingredients like sweet potatoes and tapioca for soju production. This shift democratized soju consumption, making it a staple in Korean social life. Today, soju is a global phenomenon, enjoyed for its versatility and cultural significance, symbolizing conviviality and shared moments.
