Boricha

Boricha, or barley tea, has a longstanding history deeply ingrained in Korean culture. Originating in the Three Kingdoms period (57 BCE - 668 CE), this traditional caffeine-free beverage gained popularity due to its refreshing taste and perceived health benefits. Throughout Korean history, Boricha has been cherished for its versatility, commonly served as both a hot and cold beverage. During the Joseon Dynasty (1392-1897), the tea became a staple in Korean households, appreciated for its ability to quench thirst and aid digestion. Made by roasting barley grains, Boricha holds cultural significance, often accompanying meals and serving as a gesture of hospitality. In contemporary Korea, Boricha remains a beloved and commonly consumed drink, embodying the enduring connection between tradition and modern daily life, while also symbolizing the nation's rich tea culture.
