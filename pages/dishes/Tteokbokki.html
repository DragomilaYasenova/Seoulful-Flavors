Tteokbokki

Tteokbokki, a staple in Korean cuisine, has a rich history dating back to the Joseon Dynasty. Initially known as "gungjung tteokbokki," this non-spicy version graced the royal court tables as a luxurious delicacy made with glutinous rice cakes and soy sauce. In the 1950s, the dish underwent a transformation on the streets of Seoul, with the introduction of gochujang, a fermented red chili paste, giving rise to the sweet and spicy version we know today. Tteokbokki became a popular street food, featuring chewy rice cakes bathed in the flavorful sauce. Over the years, its popularity surged, and the dish evolved with additions like fish cakes and boiled eggs. Now, tteokbokki stands as an iconic symbol of Korean street food culture, enjoyed not only in Korea but also celebrated worldwide for its unique and satisfying flavors. A serving may contain around 300-500 calories.
